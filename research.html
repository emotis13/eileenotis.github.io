Research

I study the labor through which commodities realize their value.

My research examines labor, gender, globalization, and political economy through ethnographic studies of workplaces in contemporary China. While political economists have traditionally followed commodities through production and global supply chains, I investigate what happens at the point of sale, where commodities encounter consumers and value is realized through exchange.

Realization labor

Realization labor is the labor involved in moving, stocking, displaying, explaining, transacting, and selling commodities so that value can be realized through exchange. This perspective brings political economy into the retail workplace while returning the commodity itself to the center of service-work analysis.

Internal supply chains

Retail stores are not simply endpoints of global commodity chains. They contain internal supply chains through which goods move from receiving dock to stockroom, sales floor, checkout counter, and fulfillment systems. Following commodities along these pathways reveals forms of labor often obscured in studies of both production and consumption.

Multiple labor regimes

Multiple labor regimes are arrayed along the internal supply chain. Different groups of workers encounter distinct systems of employment, control, consent, resistance, and inequality even while participating in the movement of the same commodities through a single firm.

Books and intellectual trajectory

A longstanding focus of my research is the relationship between economic transformation and inequality in contemporary China. Markets and Bodies: Women, Service Work and the Making of Inequality in China (Stanford University Press, 2011) examined how the rise of consumer markets reshaped gender, labor, and social stratification through the expansion of service employment.

My more recent work examines retail capitalism and the labor processes through which commodities realize their value. Walmart: Made in China (Stanford University Press, 2026) develops the concepts of realization labor and the internal supply chain to explain how commodities move through retail organizations and how distinct labor regimes are arrayed along those pathways. Based on ethnographic research in Walmart stores across China, the book brings together political economy, labor process theory, and the sociology of service work.

Current theoretical work

Otis, Eileen M. Forthcoming. “From the Factory Floor to the Retail Aisle: Reconstructing Burawoy’s Labor Politics in the Age of Merchant Capital.” Critical Sociology.

The article extends labor process theory beyond the factory by examining the organization of work in the sphere of realization. It reconstructs Burawoy’s framework around differentiated labor regimes arrayed along the internal supply chain.
